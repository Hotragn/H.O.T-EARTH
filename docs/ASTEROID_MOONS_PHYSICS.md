## The overriding honesty rule: comets have NO moons

The single most important honest statement of this phase, and the one the tab must
lead with, is that **comets have no moons.**

- There are **zero confirmed comet satellites.** A comet nucleus is a small,
  low-gravity body (typically a few kilometers across), too small to hold a moon in a
  stable orbit, so none has ever been found.
- The **closest real comet phenomenon is a contact binary**, which is **two lobes
  touching as a single body, not a moon**. The textbook examples are comet
  **67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko** (imaged up close by ESA Rosetta) and the Kuiper-belt
  object **Arrokoth** (imaged by NASA New Horizons). Both are one body with two lobes,
  and the tab labels them as such.
- **Fragmenting comets produce fragments, not moons.** 73P/Schwassmann-Wachmann 3
  crumbled into dozens of pieces, and Shoemaker-Levy 9 (D/1993 F2) broke into a
  "string of pearls" before striking Jupiter, but a break-up debris train is not a
  satellite.

So the tab **shows the real thing, moons of asteroids**, and it **explains the comet
situation honestly rather than inventing a comet moon.** This is the same discipline
the repo used in Phase 9 (state the real fact, never fabricate), applied here to the
question "do comets have moons?" The honest answer is no, and the tab says so.

Around that headline sit two more honest lines:

- **This is a schematic mutual-orbit view, drawn to scale, not an ephemeris.** For
  each real binary or multiple asteroid system the tab draws the primary and its moon
  or moons to scale on their **real-size, real-period mutual orbit**. What is real:
  the diameters, the separations (semi-major axes), the periods, the size ratios, and
  the DART period change. What is a convention: the orbit's orientation in space and
  the moon's along-orbit phase.
- **The DART period step change is the real highlight.** On 2022-09-26 NASA's DART hit
  Dimorphos and shortened its orbit around Didymos, the **first time humanity
  deliberately changed a celestial body's orbit**. The tab renders that before-and-
  after period as a real, measured step, the genuinely historic fact of the phase.

**These are real orbits of real moons.** The tab draws genuine geometry from published
measurements, with the schematic nature honestly stated, the real-vs-illustrative
split honestly kept, and the comet truth honestly told. It does not fake, and it does
not overclaim.

## Three structural facts that shape everything

1. **Comets have no moons; the real subject is asteroid moons.** The headline above.
   It is the reason the tab is built around real binary and multiple **asteroid**
   systems and treats comets only through an honest contact-binary and fragment note.
2. **These systems are unresolvable from Earth to the eye, so this is a
   configuration view.** They were resolved only by **radar, adaptive optics or a
   spacecraft flyby**, and there is **no full mutual-orbit ephemeris** in this tab.
   The honest headline is **what each mutual orbit looks like, to scale**, not **where
   the moon is tonight**. This is a weaker positional claim than the Dwarf Moons tab
   (Phase 17), where Pluto had real positions; here no system carries a real
   along-orbit position.
3. **Real sizes and periods, illustrative orientation and phase.** For every system,
   the diameters, separations, periods and size ratios are real and drawn to scale,
   while the orbit orientation in space and the along-orbit phase are an adopted
   convention. Keeping these apart, in the rendering and in the labels, is the central
   integrity requirement of the phase.

## COMPUTED / REAL: the substance

### The schematic mutual orbit, to scale

For each system the tab draws the primary and its moon or moons on a **mutual orbit
whose semi-major axis (separation), period and body diameters are the real, cited
values**. So the **relative sizes and the orbit scale are honest**: a small moon far
out looks small and far out, a near-equal double looks near-equal, and the orbit is as
large relative to the bodies as it really is. For a **near-equal double** (90 Antiope,
617 Patroclus + Menoetius) the two bodies are drawn about a **common barycenter placed
by the real size (mass) ratio**, because that is what the system physically is, not a
tiny moon around a big primary.

### Didymos + Dimorphos and the DART period step change (the real highlight)

**65803 Didymos + Dimorphos** is the centerpiece and the one historic fact of the
phase:

- **Didymos** is about **765 m** across and rotates in **2.2593 h**; **Dimorphos** is
  about **160 m** across; their **mutual separation** is about **1.19 km** (Daly et
  al. 2023, Nature).
- The mutual orbital period was **11.921 h before** the DART impact and **11.372 h
  after**, a shortening of **32 ± 2 minutes** (Thomas et al. 2023, Nature).
- On **2022-09-26** the DART spacecraft deliberately struck Dimorphos. This is the
  **first time humanity deliberately changed the orbit of a celestial body**, and the
  tab renders the before-and-after period as a **real, measured step change**, the
  genuine highlight.

The Didymos body carries a **reused real DART photo**; Dimorphos is illustrative.

### Ida + Dactyl and the poorly constrained orbit

**243 Ida + Dactyl** (Belton et al. 1996, Icarus) was the **first confirmed asteroid
moon**, found in Galileo flyby images in **1993**. Ida is a large, elongated main-belt
asteroid (roughly 30 km mean, more than 50 km long); Dactyl is a small (about 1.4 km)
moon. **Dactyl's orbit is the least trustworthy element in the tab:** the single
Galileo flyby could only **bound** the orbit, not solve it, so its semi-major axis and
period are genuinely uncertain and carry their own **"poorly constrained orbit"**
label, distinct from the better-measured systems, and must never be shown with false
precision. Ida carries a reused real Galileo photo; Dactyl is illustrative.

### The triple and the multiple systems

- **87 Sylvia + Romulus + Remus** (Marchis et al. 2005, Nature): the **first known
  triple asteroid**, a large main-belt primary with two small outer moons, resolved by
  adaptive optics. Drawn to real relative sizes on real mutual orbits.
- **216 Kleopatra + Alexhelios + Cleoselene** (Marchis et al. 2008; Descamps et al.
  2011): the metallic **"dog-bone"** with two small moons. The dog-bone shape is
  drawn as an illustrative shape (no shipped map); the moons are illustrative on their
  real mutual orbits.

### The near-equal doubles

- **90 Antiope** (Merline 2000; Descamps et al. 2007): a **near-equal double**, two
  similar-size components about a common barycenter, drawn as the near-equal binary it
  is with the barycenter placed by the real size ratio.
- **617 Patroclus + Menoetius**: a **Jupiter Trojan near-equal double** and a **NASA
  Lucy flyby target in 2033**, drawn the same way. Both components illustrative (Lucy
  will resolve them in 2033).

### The remaining binaries

- **22 Kalliope + Linus** (Merline et al. 2001; Margot & Brown 2003): a large primary
  with the moon Linus on its real mutual orbit; both illustrative.
- **45 Eugenia + Petit-Prince + S/2004 (45) 1** (Merline et al. 1999, Nature): Petit-
  Prince was the **first asteroid moon discovered from the ground** (adaptive optics),
  later joined by a second moon; both moons and the primary illustrative on real mutual
  orbits.

### The comet honesty note (contact binaries, fragment trains)

The tab includes an explicit note that **comets have no moons.** It shows the closest
real phenomenon, the contact binary, as **one body**: **67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko**
(reused real ESA Rosetta photo) and **Arrokoth** (illustrative two-lobe shape), both
labeled as a single body, not a moon system. It names **73P/Schwassmann-Wachmann 3**
and **Shoemaker-Levy 9 (D/1993 F2)** as **fragment trains** (break-up debris), not
satellites. The note is factual and honest, and it fabricates nothing.

### The accuracy bound (load-bearing)

- **Real and to scale:** body diameters, mutual-orbit separations (semi-major axes),
  orbital periods, size ratios, near-equal-double barycenter splits, and the Didymos
  DART period step change. Measured, cited facts.
- **Illustrative (a convention, for every system):** the orbit's **orientation in
  space** and the moon's **along-orbit phase**. There is no full mutual-orbit
  ephemeris in this tab, and these systems are unresolvable from Earth to the eye
  (radar, adaptive optics or spacecraft only), so where the orbit is tilted and where
  along it the moon currently sits are an adopted convention, not a measurement.
- **Additionally poorly constrained:** Dactyl's orbit (single flyby, bounded not
  solved).
- The UI must show this bound, must **not** present a drawn moon position as a real
  position on a date, and must **not** claim any observable transit, occultation or
  event.

## ILLUSTRATIVE / APPROXIMATE

Several rendering realities must be labeled honestly, or the picture is misleading:

- **The orbit orientation in space and the along-orbit phase are illustrative
  (adopted), for every system.** This is the central approximation of the phase. The
  orbit **size and period are real and to scale**; **how the orbit is tilted toward us
  and where the moon sits on it are a convention**, labeled distinct from the real
  sizes and periods, and never presented as a real position on a date.
- **Dactyl's orbit is additionally poorly constrained.** Single 1993 Galileo flyby,
  bounded not solved (Belton et al. 1996). It carries its own "poorly constrained
  orbit" label.
- **Rendered moon markers are enlarged for legibility.** Some moons are tiny against
  their primaries (Dimorphos is about 160 m beside 765 m Didymos; Dactyl is about
  1.4 km beside a roughly 30 km Ida). Drawn strictly to scale, such a moon would be a
  sub-pixel speck. So the app draws the **orbit geometry and the size ratio to scale**
  (that is the physics) but may **enlarge the smallest markers for visibility**, and
  must state that the on-screen dot size for the tiniest moons is a legibility choice,
  not a true angular size.
- **All moons and the un-mapped primaries are illustrative shapes.** Dimorphos,
  Dactyl, Romulus, Remus, Alexhelios, Cleoselene, Petit-Prince, S/2004 (45) 1, Linus
  and Menoetius, and the primaries Sylvia, Kleopatra, Antiope, Kalliope, Eugenia and
  Patroclus, have **no surface map** and are drawn as clearly-labeled illustrative
  shapes with no fake detail. Their **sizes and orbits are real and to scale**; only
  their appearance is illustrative. **Kleopatra's dog-bone and every irregular shape
  are illustrative**, not a resolved model.
- **Arrokoth (comet note) is an illustrative two-lobe shape** unless a map is added.
  67P is drawn from the reused real photo; Arrokoth is illustrative.
- **There are no event flags.** Unlike the Jupiter, Saturn and Other Moons tabs, this
  tab computes **no transit / shadow / occultation / eclipse flags**, because these
  systems are unresolvable from Earth and no such event is meaningfully observable. The
  tab is a schematic mutual-orbit view only, and must say so.

Nothing else is approximated and nothing is invented. There is no fabricated surface,
no faked event, no invented comet moon, and no live feed.
